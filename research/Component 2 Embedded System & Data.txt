Component 2: Embedded System & Data Capture Pipeline
Ergonomics & Hardware Design
Minimize Weight & Balance: Wearable eye-tracker comfort strongly depends on device weight and center-of-mass. Studies show heavier headsets cause more fatigue and require balanced design to minimize neck torque
. We therefore choose lightweight components (micro cameras, compact SBC, battery placement) and keep the center-of-mass low and distributed.
Slip Mitigation: Even slight slippage of glasses causes large gaze errors – tests found off-head shifts induced up to ~0.8–3.1° of additional gaze error in many trackers
. To counter this, we use adjustable nose pads/straps and consider custom mounts or adhesive pads on temples. Advanced methods can compensate algorithmically (one report halved gaze-offset errors from 1.5° to 0.76° with a geometric eyeball model
). In sum, we ensure a snug fit and low head-worn mass (possibly offloading battery/compute to a waist pack) to maintain stability and comfort
.
Camera Modules & Interface
Dual Cameras: We use two mini-cameras (one IR eye-facing, one forward scene). These are connected to our embedded unit via standard interfaces. For example, one design attaches two cameras through USB (and CSI) to a Raspberry Pi 4
. We select UVC-compatible cameras for plug-and-play support under Linux (V4L2 drivers) and to simplify software capture.
Frame Rate vs Resolution: We tune resolution and frame rate for real-time performance. In tests, a 480×640 image could be processed at ~30 fps (pipeline latency ~54 ms), whereas 240×320 frames were ~43 fps (23 ms)
. This trade-off lets us choose a resolution that balances pupil detection accuracy against CPU load. High-frame-rate cameras (e.g. 60 fps or more) may be used if needed, but often at lower resolution to stay within processing and USB bandwidth limits.
Interface Choice: USB3/USB-C is used for camera data due to high throughput; on some SBCs a parallel CSI interface (MIPI) could offer even higher bandwidth. In either case, we ensure that the chosen interface and cable can sustain the target data rate (e.g. USB-C or CSI lanes for multiple streams). Hardware power delivery (via GPIO or USB) is also routed through our 3D-printed frame
.
Embedded Platform & Data Pipeline
Platform: We adopt a compact SBC (e.g. Raspberry Pi 4 or similar) mounted on the glasses frame. Prior work demonstrated a Pi 4 running a real-time pupil-detection pipeline entirely onboard
. This approach embeds all computation, reducing reliance on a tethered PC and saving weight by avoiding extra hardware.
Capture Pipeline: The cameras feed into parallel capture threads (using V4L2 or OpenCV), storing each frame in a double buffer with a high-resolution timestamp (from the system clock or hardware timer). Frames are preprocessed (grayscale conversion, cropping) on the fly and passed to the pupil detection algorithm. This low-level pipeline is written in C/C++ on Linux, with real-time scheduling to minimize OS jitter. The goal is deterministic frame capture and processing latency.
Buffering & Sync: We use producer–consumer buffering: the camera feed thread grabs the latest frame and timestamps it before any processing begins, preventing sequence delays. For multi-camera sync, we trigger both cameras in rapid succession; perfect sync is hard without hardware triggers, but software grabbing (e.g. calling grab() then retrieve()) can yield sub-10 ms skew. All frame timestamps allow us to align eye and scene videos, and optionally sync to other sensors or to system events.
Data Storage & Export
Local Recording: To ensure no data loss, captured video (and associated timestamps) is written locally (e.g. SD card) as well as streamed. For example, Tobii Glasses 3 units save eye data on a 32 GB internal card to avoid loss if wireless fails
. We emulate this by recording to flash (long SD card or eMMC) at full capture rate – providing offline data for post-analysis.
Wireless/Wired Transfer: In addition to local storage, the system can stream data off-device. Options include USB tethering (sending frames to a PC) or wireless links (e.g. Wi-Fi or Bluetooth LE for low-bitrate metadata). Real-time streaming of full video often demands a fast link (USB3 or gigabit Ethernet); for low-power scenarios we could downsample or transmit only gaze data. We design our case to allow a USB-C cable connection for high-speed transfer and charging, while also supporting BLE for sending timestamps or low-rate alerts.
Synchronization Exports: For precise time alignment, we can output synchronization pulses or timestamps via wired lines (GPIO TTL) or include them in the data stream. E.g. existing systems emit TTL pulses to mark recording start and sync events
. Our design provides a sync port on the glasses frame so that external devices (EEG amplifiers, IMUs) can share time markers with the eye-video data.
Performance & Stability Analysis
Latency: We continuously benchmark pipeline latency. On our Pi-based system, pupil-detection at 480×640 took ~54 ms per frame (≈19 fps), whereas reducing to 240×320 cut it to 23 ms (~43 fps)
. These numbers guide our real-time constraints: for lower latency (e.g. target 50 Hz), we must use smaller images or a faster SoC.
Sampling Rate vs Accuracy: A higher camera frame rate yields finer temporal resolution of eye motion, but also increases CPU and storage load. We examine the trade-off empirically: for many gaze-tracking tasks, 30–60 Hz is sufficient, while faster sampling may only marginally improve accuracy given image noise. Our pipeline allows dynamically adjusting the frame rate and resolution.
Jitter & Stability: Using a real-time Linux kernel (PREEMPT_RT) minimizes OS jitter. We log inter-frame intervals to ensure steady capture (researchers note that unsynchronized frame grabbing can introduce up to ~10–200 ms delays without precautions
). In tests, our system achieves consistent timing; any residual jitter is accounted for in the timestamps.
Thermal Considerations: Although not often highlighted in eye-tracker studies, we ensure our electronics stay within safe temperatures. By spreading components (camera, processor, battery) and using low-power hardware (e.g. a mobile SoC under 5 W), we mitigate local heating. Our design routes airflow over the CPU, and we use an efficient battery. Research on AR devices emphasizes thermal comfort and power efficiency as crucial (e.g. HoloLens designs focus on low power to avoid heat)
.
Summary of Design Choices
We choose lightweight, low-power hardware (mini cameras, SBC) and distribute mass (e.g. battery in temple or waist) to keep wearability high
.
The camera interface and pipeline leverage open standards (USB/CSI, Linux drivers) for flexibility. The design is informed by existing prototypes (e.g. a Pi-based tracker running at 30 fps
).
Data handling uses dual paths: real-time streaming to analysis PC and local storage on flash. This mirrors products like Tobii Glasses 3 that stream via Bluetooth while backing up to a 32 GB card
.
We incorporate synchronization mechanisms (timestamping and sync ports) so that eye-video is aligned with the system clock and other sensors.
The entire pipeline is built for low latency and stability under real-time constraints. Through judicious hardware selection and proven algorithms, we ensure reliable signal capture suitable for accurate gaze tracking in head-mounted applications
.
