1. Ergonomics, Wearability, and Thermal/Slippage Constraints
These papers investigate why lightweight, well-balanced physical designs are mandatory for head-worn devices. They provide excellent justification for your decision to offload the heavy components (battery and CPU) into a separate waist/pocket unit.

"Effects of the Weight and Balance of Head-Mounted Displays on Physical Load"
Link: https://www.mdpi.com/2076-3417/11/15/6802#:~:text=Afterwards%2C%20the%20participants%20completed%20questionnaires,of%20weight%20during%20HMD%20design

"The impact of slippage on the data quality of head-worn eye trackers"
Authors: Niehorster, D. C., et al. (2020)
Context: A seminal paper describing how even minor shifts in head-unit weight distribution (often caused by speaking or head movements) create "slippage" that completely ruins angular calibration over time. It compares various setups like Tobii and Pupil Labs.
Link / Access: 10.3758/s13428-019-01307-0 (Open Access PDF usually available on Springer).

"Slippage-robust Gaze Tracking for Near-eye Display"
Authors: Li, J., et al. (2020)
Context: Introduces technical and physical methods to compensate for slippage by using geometric eyeball modeling when the heavy glasses shift on the inspector's face.
Link / Access: Available via IEEE Xplore or ResearchGate.



2. Embedded Systems & Edge Processing Pipelines
These academic papers focus on the software/hardware co-design necessary to fetch frames from cameras, process them in real-time, and manage power footprints on embedded SoCs.

Combining EEG and eye tracking:
a workflow for your mobile experiment
Link: https://pressrelease.brainproducts.com/eeg-eyetracking-mobile/

"An Embedded and Real-Time Pupil Detection Pipeline"
Authors: Ankur Raj, Diwas Bhattarai, and Kristof Van Laerhoven (2023)
Context: Demonstrates deploying lightweight models and pipelines on resource-constrained embedded targets. Highlights the limits of edge devices while pushing 30Hz+ frame-rate processing.
Link / Access: arXiv:2302.14098 (Free PDF directly on arXiv).


"PuRe: Robust pupil detection for real-time pervasive eye tracking"
Authors: Santini, T., Fuhl, W., & Kasneci, E. (2018)
Context: Offers core technical strategies regarding how raw camera signals are efficiently captured and segmented in real-time, focusing heavily on standard C++ implementations rather than heavy deep-learning frameworks (justifying your baseline CV approaches).
Link / Access: Available via ScienceDirect / ResearchGate.


"OpenEyes: A low-cost head-mounted eye-tracking solution"
Authors: Li, D., Babcock, J., & Parkhurst, D. J. (2006)
Context: While older, this is heavily referenced. It outlines the foundational architecture of decoupling the capture unit (USB cameras) from the computing hardware processing the algorithms.
Link / Access: Searchable via Google Scholar (often freely hosted PDF).


3. Commercial Architecture Specifications (Industry Reference)
It is highly recommended to reference how multi-million dollar eye-tracking companies solve the same problems your team is facing.

Tobii Pro Glasses 3 Product Specifications / Whitepapers
Architecture Detail: Tobii strictly utilizes a distributed physical architecture. The head unit (containing 4 eye cameras, IR LEDs, and 1 Scene camera) weighs just 76.5 grams. All recording, battery supply (18650 Li-ion), and processing happen on a standalone 312-gram Recording Unit tethered via cable.
Why you should cite it: argument for creating a separate case system with an embedded processor and a 5-6 hour battery, rather than shoving everything onto the user's face.
Link / Access: Tobii Pro Glasses 3 Official Site (Specification brochures available directly on the page).


Pupil Labs "Invisible"
Architecture Detail: Rather than building a custom embedded recorder like Tobii, Pupil Labs relies completely on an Android Companion Device (such as a tethered OnePlus smartphone) for edge computation. The glasses are merely dumb USB-C sensor hubs, while the phone handles the Deep Learning algorithm processing.
Why you should cite it: Demonstrates that even the most modern CV/Deep Learning eye-tracking products require external edge processing devices due to thermal and battery efficiency limits.
Link / Access: Pupil Invisible Whitepaper (Accessible natively via their website).


How to best utilize these in your presentation: When discussing your specific hardware choices, cite Niehorster (2020) to prove why having a lightweight frame avoids calibration slippage. Afterwards, use Tobii Pro Glasses 3 and Pupil Invisible as "Industry validation" for why your team chose to tether lightweight USB cameras to an external embedded case. Both are standard, indisputable defenses for your design architecture!
