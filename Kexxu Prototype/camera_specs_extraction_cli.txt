/dev/media1
That is the Media Controller node. It’s used by the system to map the hardware topology (how the sensor connects to the processing unit). You don't need to worry about it for your ML project; OpenCV and FFmpeg only care about the /dev/videoX capture node.

v4l2-ctl -d /dev/eye --set-ctrl=auto_exposure=1 --set-ctrl=exposure_time_absolute=157
v4l2-ctl -d /dev/front --set-ctrl=auto_exposure=1 --set-ctrl=exposure_time_absolute=166

For the Eye Camera (157):
    157×100μs=15.7ms.
    Since your target is 60 fps, your frame interval is exactly 16.66ms.
    Logic: By setting it to 15.7ms, you are leaving a small "safety buffer" (~1ms) for the ISP to package the data and send it over the USB bus without missing the next 16.6ms window .  

For the Front Camera (166):
    166×100μs=16.6ms.
    Since your target is 30 fps, your frame interval is 33.33ms
    Logic: This is a very fast shutter speed for 30 fps. It results in sharp, crisp "world-view" frames with almost zero motion blur, which is ideal for identifying distant objects or text in an industrial environment.
    
If your image is too dark after running these commands, do not increase the exposure time (as it will drop your FPS). Instead, increase the Gain (analog amplification):
v4l2-ctl -d /dev/eye --set-ctrl=gain=50s
